### Response

```txt
Sonnet: The Paradox of Strife

In tempest’s wake, where thunder rends the air,
A fragile calm may cradle Earth’s scarred face;
For war, though fierce, at times lays poison bare,
And peace, like dawn, ascends from violence’s grace.

The sword, once forged to sunder bonds of trust,
Becomes the plow that breaks the fallow ground,
As nations weep for ash and faded dust,
Then plant frail seeds where harmony is found.

Yet shadows linger where the cannons roared—
Each truce a thread spun thin by hands still bruised.
What wisdom blooms from fields by blood restored?
What fragile treaties leave the soul confused?

For war and peace, twin flames in cruel embrace,
Spin time’s old wheel with neither haste nor pause—
One carves the path, the other fills the space,
Both bound by laws that none may dare to parse.

So history’s page, inked red yet edged with light,
Proclaims this truth: From ends of wrath, beginnings take flight.
```

This sonnet grapples with war’s paradoxical role in shaping peace, weaving imagery of destruction and renewal while questioning the cost. The volta in the final couplet suggests that peace, though born of violence, carries the weight of its origins.
